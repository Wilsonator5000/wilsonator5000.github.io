The Plight of the Rohingya

January 29, 2018

Patricia Smith Published January 18, 2018

"A minority group of Muslims has been violently driven out of Myanmar in what experts are calling a case of ethnic cleansing."

Subject & Background

This article explains Myanmar's violent attempt to wipe out the entire population of a minority, Muslim group called the Rohingya, and the struggles of thousands of Muslims trying to escape.

On right: A map of Myanmar.

Above: A group of Rohingya Muslims facing violence in Myanmar.

All across the country of Myanmar, thousands of members of the Rohingya Muslim group are being brutally beaten and killed, their villages burned to the ground. Even though the Rohingya have lived in Myanmar for centuries, they are still a minority group and make up less than a fiftieth of the country's population, which is the probable reason for their persecution (most Myanmar citizens are Buddhist).

Over the past few months, Myanmar's military has become involved in the genocide as well, stating that they are "acting in response to attacks carried out by the Rohingya" (Smith 13). This "response", however, has quickly became what is known as ethnic cleansing when a religious group or race are killed with the hope of destroying the entire population (most notably the Holocaust of the Jews by the Nazis in World War II). Myanmar's leader, Aung San Suu Kyi, has made no attempt to stop the military presence over the Rohingya, despite winning the Nobel Peace Prize in 1991 and being a longtime advocate of democracy.

"'This is the "never again" scenario [...] [but] here we are 75 years later, and there's another stateless people who are being denied their citizenship and attacked.'"

John Sifton of Human Rights Watch

Over 620,000 Rohingya have fled Myanmar to safety in Bangladesh, a primarily Muslim country, where they have been welcomed by the country's prime minister Sheikh Hasina; but human rights groups say that communities from around the world must demand justice for the Rohingya.

Above: Protests against Myanmar's leader Aung San Suu Kyi to stop the genocide.

Occasion & Purpose

Despite the United Nations' "never again" initiative to ensure that a mass genocide like that of the Holocaust of the Jews never happens again, it seems that there are new incidents of ethnic cleansing several times a year, occurring through acts of violence in countries large and small.

This article was likely written to inform readers of yet another instance of genocide towards a religious group, and persuade them to advocate for human rights by explaining both the severity of the situation and then how they can help (see right).

"It's critical that the international community speaks out and demands justice for the Rohingya."

Tone

"'But [the military] didn't stop; [...] they chopped people, they shot people, they raped us, they left us senseless.'"

Rajuma, a Rohingya survivor

The author, Patricia Smith, uses stark examples of the violence experienced by the Rohingya Muslims, including quotes from people who have faced the persecution firsthand (see quote, left). This creates a vivid picture in the reader's mind of Myanmar's initiated genocide, which may persuade them to advocate against it (the purpose of the article).

Further Analysis

How are the struggles of the Rohingya similar to those faced by Jews in the 1930s & 1940s? How is their struggle different? Give specific evidence.

The struggles of the Rohingya Muslims share many similarities to that of the Jews in the 1930s and 1940s during World War II. For example, both instances involve a government body attempting to remove a group from their country in the Holocaust of the Jews in World War II, the Nazi military attempted to exterminate the Jews from Germany (and eventually the world), while in the attacks on the Rohingya, the Myanmar military is attempting to exterminate the Rohingya Muslims from their country.

Another way that the struggles of the Rohingya and the Jews are similar is in the conditions that members of the groups experienced while being attacked and trying to escape. In the Holocaust, Jewish communities were destroyed and individuals were sent off to Nazi death camps, much like in Myanmar where Rohingya villages are burned and individuals are killed on the spot.

One way that the struggles of the Rohingya and the Jews are different is in the way that they are organized. In the Holocaust, Nazi leader Adolf Hitler created a widespread, methodical plan for exterminating the Jews through the creation of concentration, work, and death camps. In the attacks on the Rohingya, however, the attacks are limited to random outbreaks by the military and mobs of anti-minority citizens.
